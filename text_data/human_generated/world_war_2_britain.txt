A very misleading question, probably designed to be misleading. No country, not even Nazi Germany or the USSR, conscripted “most” of its population during World War II.

In World War II, conscription was mandatory for all males as soon as they turned 18 years old. My grandmother took in several orphaned boys from East London, during the Blitz, and they all grew up and were conscripted as the War went on. This was typical.

If you tried to avoid the draft, you faced serious criminal charges, and could be imprisoned. Some men did try to hide and avoid conscription, but not many. There were “conshies,” too, or COs, conscientious objectors, about 60,000 of them. These men (and some women) were required to perform civilian war work at home, or were drafted and trained for non-combat service as determined by a tribunal. Those who were serious, refusing to do any service, faced imprisonment. They were regarded as pariahs on a par with Moseley’s little gang of numpties.

The UK, like most belligerents in World War II, had a variety of categories for those not subject to conscription. Most married women and widows with children were excused war work because their “duty” was to their families. Young, unmarried women without children were expected to perform war work, either in one of the Women’s Auxiliary services, or in the Land Army, or a war industry. Many performed clerical duties, drove trucks on the home front, etc.

Men in reserved occupations were also not conscripted. “Reserved occupations” were those considered vital to the maintenance of society - doctors, vets, teachers, policemen, firemen, etc. Reserved occupations also included many skilled factory workers, farmers, fishermen, and others whose labor supported the war effort directly or indirectly. Holding one of these jobs didn’t mean you wouldn’t be drafted, though. If you were considered “surplus to requirements,” and young enough, you might be drafted, but often you were placed in a similar role in the armed forces, where your civilian expertise was needed.

Take academics, for instance, my own profession. In World War II Britain, being a historian, I would have been drafted, probably (except that I’m too old), but I’d probably be assigned a military intelligence role, or assigned as a military educator. The British services conducted a lot of schooling to quickly get personnel up to speed in a variety of topics. Needless to say, I probably wouldn’t be teaching history for the duration.

It simply wasn’t possible to avoid conscription in wartime Britain for any length of time. To do so, you would have to drop off the radar, go without a ration book, find someone willing to risk the social stigma of hiding you, and the inconvenience of sharing their rations. You wouldn’t be able to travel, go out in public, or work. If you were a conscription-age young man, hanging around any town, village, or city, and not in uniform, or doing war work, the police might want to have a word with you. You were going to stand out very conspicuously. In some countries, people dodge the draft and don’t have to worry about enforcement much, or they can buy their way out if they have money to bribe officials or pay fines. In wartime Britain the average citizen didn’t tolerate shirking. Everyone was working and sacrificing so much that they resented anyone who wasn’t pulling their weight.

There were about 100,000 deserters from the British forces during World War II, who were caught and punished, but this amounts to only 2% of the 5 or so million men who served, so although it’s a significant number, it wasn’t a catastrophic level of desertion. Other armies suffered much worse levels of desertion. U.S. desertion rates were comparatively low, about 50,000 altogether, half of whom were convicted, but American troops had fewer opportunities to desert and make it home, while trying to hide, as an American, in a liberated city was a difficult life. You were almost obligated to support yourself by joining a criminal gang of other deserters, which sort of defeats the whole purpose of desertion.
